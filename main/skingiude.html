Skin Guide

Erythemato-squamous diseases are a group of skin conditions characterized by both redness (erythema) and scaling (squamous) of the skin. These conditions typically involve inflammation and shedding of the outermost layer of the skin (epidermis), resulting in symptoms such as redness, itching, and flaking. Erythemato-squamous diseases encompass a range of dermatological conditions, each with its own distinct characteristics, causes, and treatments.They all share the clinical features of erythema and scaling, with minimal differences. The disorders in this group are psoriasis, seborrheic dermatitis, lichen planus, pityriasis rosea, chronic dermatitis, and pityriasis rubra pilaris. The family history feature has the value 1 if any of these diseases has been observed in the family, and 0 otherwise.Every other feature clinical and histopathological was given a degree in the range of 0 to 3. Here, 0 indicates that the feature was not present, 3 indicates the largest amount possible, and 1, 2 indicate the relative intermediate values.

Psoriasis
Psoriasis is a chronic autoimmune condition that causes red, scaly patches or plaques to develop on the skin. It is characterized by rapid turnover of skin cells, resulting in thick, silvery scales and inflammation. Psoriasis can affect any part of the body and may be associated with joint pain (psoriatic arthritis).It occurs when the immune system mistakenly attacks healthy skin cells, leading to rapid turnover of skin cells and the formation of thick, silvery scales.
Seborrheic Dermatitis
Seborrheic dermatitis is a common inflammatory skin condition characterized by redness, greasy scales, and itching, typically affecting areas rich in oil glands such as the scalp, face (particularly the eyebrows, eyelids, and nasolabial folds), and upper chest. It is believed to be caused by an overgrowth of yeast (Malassezia) on the skin, along with genetic and environmental factors.
Lichen Planus
Lichen planus is a chronic inflammatory condition that affects the skin, mucous membranes, hair, and nails. It typically presents as small, flat-topped, shiny, purplish bumps (papules) or patches with fine white lines (Wickham's striae) on the surface. Lichen planus can cause itching, pain, and changes in skin texture, and its exact cause is unknown, although it may involve autoimmune or allergic mechanisms.
Pityriasis Rosea
Pityriasis rosea is an acute, self-limiting skin condition characterized by the sudden onset of a single, large, pink or red patch (herald patch) followed by smaller, scaly patches that spread symmetrically over the trunk, arms, and legs in a Christmas tree-like pattern. The exact cause of pityriasis rosea is unknown, but it may be associated with viral infections, particularly human herpesvirus 6 (HHV-6) and human herpesvirus 7 (HHV-7).
Chronic Dermatitis
Chronic dermatitis refers to long-lasting inflammation of the skin. Dermatitis itself is a broad term used to describe various skin conditions characterized by redness, itching, swelling, and sometimes blistering or scaling of the skin. When dermatitis persists for an extended period, it is termed chronic.Treatment for chronic dermatitis depends on the underlying cause and may include topical corticosteroids, moisturizers, antihistamines, and avoiding triggers or irritants. In severe cases, systemic medications or phototherapy may be necessary.
Pityriasis Rubra Pilaris
Pityriasis rubra pilaris (PRP) is a rare and chronic skin disorder characterized by reddish-orange patches, scaling, and thickening of the skin, often accompanied by small follicular bumps. It typically starts with red, scaly patches on the skin that may spread to cover larger areas of the body. The condition may also affect the palms of the hands and soles of the feet.While there is no cure for PRP, treatment aims to alleviate symptoms and may include topical therapies, oral medications such as retinoids or immunosuppressants, phototherapy, and supportive measures such as moisturizers and gentle skincare. The condition tends to be chronic and may wax and wane over time, with periods of exacerbation and remission.
